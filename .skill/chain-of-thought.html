      Chain-of-Thought (CoT)

      A reasoning technique that instructs the model to produce explicit intermediate steps before its final answer. Documented to significantly improve accuracy on multi-step arithmetic, logic, planning, and code analysis tasks — but only under specific model-scale and task-type conditions. Two deployment variants are provided below: Zero-Shot CoT and Few-Shot CoT.

      chain-of-thought zero-shot CoT few-shot CoT reasoning multi-step thought-generation production

      Research basis & prerequisites

      Chain-of-Thought prompting was formally introduced by Wei et al. (2022). The zero-shot variant (“Let’s think step by step”) was demonstrated by Kojima et al. (2022). Both papers establish a hard model-scale prerequisite: measurable accuracy gains from CoT occur only above approximately 100B parameters. Below that threshold — including Llama 3.1 8B, Mistral 7B, and most fine-tuned small models — CoT produces no improvement or actively degrades performance (Tianpan, 2026). On frontier API-tier models (GPT-4 class, Claude 3.5+, Gemini 1.5+), CoT is effective.

      A 2025 study on Qwen2.5 models found that zero-shot CoT matches or beats few-shot CoT on arithmetic and logic tasks, because modern instruction-tuned models weight the instruction and test question more heavily than in-context exemplars. This means on frontier models, Zero-Shot CoT is the correct default; Few-Shot CoT is reserved for tasks where a correct reasoning-trace exemplar is unavailable to the model from pre-training (highly domain-specific notation, custom schemas).

      When CoT applies (decision criteria)

      Task complexity
      Multi-step arithmetic, logical deduction chains, SQL construction from ambiguous specs, planning with ordered dependencies. CoT shows no measurable gain on classification, extraction, or structured retrieval — use RISEN for those.
      Model scale
      100B+ parameters or frontier API-tier (GPT-4, Claude 3.5+, Gemini 1.5+). Below this threshold, use few-shot examples or explicit step instructions in the system prompt instead.
      Error rate
      CoT is worth the token cost when your zero-shot baseline error rate is high enough that a 10–15 percentage-point improvement clears your SLA. If baseline accuracy is already above 85–90%, CoT rarely justifies its overhead.
      Latency budget
      CoT inflates token output by 2–5×, adding multiple seconds of latency. If your SLA is sub-second, CoT is ineligible regardless of accuracy gains. Evaluate Chain-of-Draft (see below) as an alternative.

      Variant 1 — Worked Zero-Shot CoT example

      A fully-instantiated prompt and its expected output shape. The Final answer: heading creates a reliable extraction point for downstream parsing.

      ## Role
You are a senior data engineer with expertise in SQL and database performance.

## Task
Determine the correct SQL query to return each department’s name and the
number of employees who joined after 2020-01-01, but only for departments
that have more than 5 such employees.

## Reasoning instructions
Think through this step by step before giving your final answer.
For each step:
  1. State what you are determining.
  2. Show your reasoning or working.
  3. State the intermediate conclusion.

After completing all steps, write your final answer under the exact heading:

**Final answer:**

## Narrowing
- Do NOT skip steps or compress multiple steps into one.
- Do NOT state the final answer before the reasoning is complete.
- Do NOT speculate beyond the information provided.

---
[PROBLEM BEGINS BELOW THIS LINE]

Schema:
  departments(id INT, name VARCHAR)
  employees(id INT, dept_id INT, hire_date DATE)

---

[EXPECTED OUTPUT SHAPE]

Step 1: Determining what I need to count.
I need to count employees per department where hire_date > 2020-01-01.
Intermediate conclusion: I need a filtered aggregate.

Step 2: Determining how to join the tables.
Join employees to departments on dept_id = departments.id.
Intermediate conclusion: INNER JOIN departments ON employees.dept_id = departments.id.

Step 3: Applying the filter.
hire_date > ‘2020-01-01’ goes in WHERE.
Intermediate conclusion: WHERE e.hire_date > ‘2020-01-01’.

Step 4: Applying the HAVING clause.
I need departments with more than 5 qualifying employees, so after GROUP BY,
HAVING COUNT(*) > 5.
Intermediate conclusion: Use HAVING, not WHERE, because the condition applies
to an aggregate.

**Final answer:**
SELECT d.name, COUNT(e.id) AS employee_count
FROM employees e
INNER JOIN departments d ON e.dept_id = d.id
WHERE e.hire_date > ‘2020-01-01’
GROUP BY d.id, d.name
HAVING COUNT(e.id) > 5;

      Alternatives when CoT is too expensive

      • Chain-of-Draft (CoD). Generates minimal, abbreviated intermediate reasoning annotations rather than verbose step-by-step prose. Achieves accuracy comparable to standard CoT while using 75–80% fewer tokens. Benchmark before committing to standard CoT in any cost-sensitive pipeline (reported in production decision literature, Tianpan 2026).
      • Token-budget-aware reasoning. Instruct the model explicitly: “Reason in no more than N words before your final answer.” Studies show this cuts output tokens by 60–70% with negligible accuracy loss on reasoning tasks. Simpler to implement than structural CoT changes and often equally effective.
      • Plan-and-Solve (PS+). Instructs the model to first generate a plan, then execute it step-by-step. Outperforms standard Zero-Shot CoT on arithmetic reasoning benchmarks (Wang et al., 2023 — ACL 2023). Structure: “Let’s first understand the problem and devise a plan, then carry out the plan step by step.”

      When NOT to use CoT

      • Model scale below ~100B parameters. CoT shows no measurable accuracy gain and may degrade performance. Use few-shot examples or explicit system prompt instructions instead.
      • Classification, extraction, or retrieval tasks. CoT’s gains over zero-shot are statistically indistinguishable on these task types. RISEN is the right tool.
      • Latency-sensitive pipelines (sub-second SLA). CoT adds seconds; evaluate Chain-of-Draft or token-budget-aware reasoning first.
      • High baseline accuracy (>85–90%). The token cost multiplier (2–5×) is unlikely to be justified by the marginal accuracy improvement at this baseline level.
      • When you cannot verify intermediate steps. If the reasoning trace cannot be audited (e.g., fully automated pipelines with no human review), CoT’s main advantage — exposing where the model’s reasoning fails — is unrealised. Use structured output prompting instead.
